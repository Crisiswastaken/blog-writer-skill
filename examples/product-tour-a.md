"Why your product tour is invisible" (written following the skill)

Nobody reads your product tour.
That's not a hot take — it's what your analytics show if you look at the right numbers. Drop-off at step two. Tooltip clicks that don't lead anywhere. Users who completed onboarding and still couldn't find the core feature three days later. The tour ran, technically. Nobody watched.
The standard explanation is that users are lazy or impatient. That's wrong. Users will sit through a 45-minute onboarding call. They'll read a 3,000-word doc if it answers the question they actually have. The problem isn't attention span. It's that product tours are almost always built to serve the product team's anxiety, not the user's actual confusion.
Think about what drives the decision to build a tour. Usually it's a metric: activation rate is low, users aren't reaching the "aha moment," support tickets are piling up about a feature that's been live for six months. So someone opens Driver.js or Appcues, draws a box around the relevant UI elements, writes some tooltip copy that sounds helpful, and ships it. Problem solved.
Except the user who just signed up doesn't have the anxiety the product team has. They have a different question entirely, one they formed in the thirty seconds between clicking "Start Trial" and landing in your app. They want to know: "Can this actually do what I came here for?" The tour is answering a question they didn't ask.
This is the thing most product teams get backwards. Onboarding isn't a tour of your product. It's a path to the user's first success. Those are completely different destinations.
A tour says: here's the dashboard, here's the menu, here's where you configure X. It's a real estate walkthrough for a house the user hasn't decided to rent yet. A success path says: you came here to do Y. Here's the fastest route to doing Y. It skips the spare bedroom entirely.
The fix isn't complicated, but it requires something product teams resist: cutting things. Most tours try to show everything because everything was built for a reason and someone will be upset if it gets skipped. But a user who just signed up cannot absorb seven steps of context. They'll click through your checklist at the speed of obligation and retain nothing.
Pick the one action that makes a user say "okay, this actually works." The single irreducible thing. For a project management tool it might be creating and assigning a task. For an analytics platform it might be seeing their own data on a chart for the first time. Everything before that moment is setup. Everything after is exploration. Your tour should only exist to get them to that moment — and then get out of the way.
The other thing worth saying: timing matters more than content. A tour that fires the second someone lands in your app is almost always wrong. They just clicked a button. They don't know what they don't know yet. The right trigger is the moment of first confusion — which you can identify, roughly, by looking at where people first stop moving. Where the cursor idles. Where the session ends. Put your help there, not at the welcome screen.
None of this is technically hard. Driver.js is well-documented. Appcues has templates. The hard part is convincing yourself to show less, say less, and trust that a user who achieves one real thing in their first session is more likely to come back than one who sat through your entire guided tour and left having done nothing at all.
