Nobody reads your product tour.

I don't mean they skim it. I mean they treat it the way you treat cookie consent banners: click, click, click, gone. The tooltip hasn't finished rendering before their finger is on "Next."

Look at your step-two drop-off. Not the completion rate, which lies. The number of users who "finished" onboarding and then opened a support ticket within three days asking how to do the thing the tour just showed them. That number tells you everything.

Product teams hear this and blame attention spans. But the same user who clicked through your seven-step tooltip sequence sat through a 45-minute sales demo to get here. They read your pricing page, FAQ included. They configured their Slack notifications. Attention isn't the problem.

The tour is answering a question nobody asked.

A user who just signed up has one question, and it formed in the gap between clicking "Start Free Trial" and seeing your dashboard. It goes something like: *can this thing do the thing I came here for?* That's it. Your tour is pointing out the settings menu.

I keep thinking about a team I worked with, I think it was late 2022 or early 2023. Analytics product, the kind with nine tabs on first login. They'd built this onboarding sequence that went on forever. Twelve steps, maybe fourteen. I don't remember exactly. What I remember is sitting in on a session recording where a user clicked "Next" on every single tooltip without reading any of them, then closed the tab. The whole thing took about forty seconds.

Their completion rate was something like 23%, and the PM was genuinely proud of that because it used to be lower.

We cut it to two steps. Connect your data source, see your first chart. That was it. Activation climbed, I want to say thirty-something percent over the following month. I don't have the exact number in front of me, but it was significant enough that the PM Slacked me about it at 11pm, which I remember because I was already in bed.

The instinct to show everything is hard to fight. I get it. Every feature was somebody's quarter. The nav redesign took six sprints. The integrations page is actually impressive if someone would just *look*. But here's the problem: a new user cannot absorb seven steps of context about a product they haven't committed to yet. They'll click through at the speed of obligation and retain nothing.

Pick one action. That's the whole strategy, really.

The single thing that makes a new user think "okay, this works." For a project management tool, probably creating a task and assigning it. For an email platform, sending a test campaign. For a design tool, dragging something onto a canvas and watching it render. Everything before that moment is setup cost. Everything after is exploration the user does on their own, because now they trust the product.

Timing matters too, and this part is almost always wrong.

A tour that fires on first login is interruption. The user just arrived. They haven't had a chance to be confused yet. Your help isn't help, it's noise. The better trigger is the first moment of hesitation. Session recordings show you where that is: the cursor stops moving, hovers without clicking, or the session just ends. That's where your guidance should appear. Not at the welcome screen. At the point of friction.

I used to work with a designer who said "if you have to explain it, you've already lost." That's too strong for complex products (sometimes explanation is genuinely necessary), but the spirit is right. The best onboarding doesn't feel like a tour. It feels like the product just... works. The tooltip appears right when you were about to get stuck. The empty state tells you what to do instead of staring at you. The first experience is built around doing, not watching.

The tooling isn't the hard part. Driver.js is well-documented. Appcues has templates. The hard part is resisting the urge to show someone your whole house when they just want to know where the kitchen is.
