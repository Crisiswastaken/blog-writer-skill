Nobody finishes your product tour.

The ones who click through every tooltip, maybe 23% on a good day, will open a support ticket tomorrow asking how to do the thing the tour just showed them. That number should terrify product teams more than it does.

I watched this happen up close. I was working with an analytics company, sometime in late 2022 I think, and they'd built this onboarding sequence that must have been twelve or fourteen steps long. I sat in on a session recording where a user clicked "Next" on every single tooltip without reading any of them, then closed the tab. Forty seconds, start to finish.

The PM showed me their completion numbers like they were good news.

The instinct, when you see those numbers, is to fix the tour. Better copy. Better highlighting. Maybe some animation to grab attention. This is the wrong instinct. You're polishing a thing that shouldn't exist in its current form.

When someone signs up for your product, they've just committed a tiny act of faith. Clicked "Start Free Trial," handed over an email address, waited for the dashboard to load. They have exactly one question, and it isn't "where's the settings menu." It's closer to: *can this thing do what I need it to do?* Your fourteen-step tooltip sequence is answering a question nobody asked.

A tour is directions to every room in the house. A success path is directions to the kitchen. One assumes the user wants to understand your product. The other assumes they want to accomplish something. **The second assumption is almost always correct.**

That analytics company. We cut their tour to two steps: connect your data source, see a chart. Activation jumped 41% in the first month. Not because the product changed. Because we stopped asking new users to care about nine tabs before they'd seen a single result. The PM Slacked me that number at 11pm on a Tuesday, which I remember because I'd already put my phone on the charger.

The difficult part isn't knowing this. The difficult part is cutting.

Every feature in that tour was somebody's quarter. The integrations page alone took six sprints. There's a real cost to telling a PM that the thing they built doesn't make the first-run experience, and most onboarding projects avoid that conversation entirely. They default to showing everything. Which is the same as showing nothing, because a new user cannot absorb seven steps of context about a product they haven't decided to keep yet.

Pick one action. The single thing that makes a user think "okay, this works." For a project management tool, it's probably creating a task and assigning it. For an email platform, sending a test campaign. Everything before that moment is cost. Everything after is the user's own curiosity.

Timing matters too, and most teams get it backwards.

A tour that fires the moment someone logs in is interruption, not help. The user just arrived. They haven't had a chance to be confused yet. The better trigger is the first moment of hesitation. You can find it in session recordings: the cursor stops moving, hovers without clicking, or the session just ends. Put your guidance there, not at the welcome screen. At the friction.

I used to work with a designer who'd say "if you have to explain it, you already lost." Too strong for complex products (sometimes you genuinely need to explain), but the impulse is right. The best onboarding doesn't feel like a tour. It feels like the product just works. The tooltip shows up the moment you were about to get stuck. The empty state tells you what to do. The first experience is built around doing, not watching.

Driver.js and Appcues handle the implementation. The tooling was never the problem. The problem is believing that a user who does one real thing in their first session is worth more than a user who sat through your entire guided tour and left having done nothing at all.
