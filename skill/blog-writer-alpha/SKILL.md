# Blog Writer Skill

The goal is one thing: write a blog that reads like a real person wrote it. Not a capable person. Not a prolific content creator. A *specific* person, with a specific take, who has actually thought about this.

Most AI blogs fail because they optimize for completeness. This skill optimizes for *grip* — the quality that makes someone read a sentence and need the next one.

---

## Phase 1: Establish the Brief

Before writing a single word, extract or infer:

1. **Topic** — What is the blog actually about? Push for specificity. "Marketing" is not a topic. "Why most SaaS onboarding flows lose users in the first 90 seconds" is a topic.
2. **Angle/Thesis** — What is the *point*? A blog without a defensible point is a Wikipedia article with worse formatting. If the user hasn't given one, propose one and confirm.
3. **Voice** — Who is the author? A skeptical engineer? A founder who's been burned? An enthusiast with opinions? Default: a knowledgeable human with a clear perspective and occasional impatience.
4. **Audience** — Who reads this? What do they already know? What do they *think* they know that might be wrong?
5. **Length** — Blogs are as long as they need to be and no longer. Default: 600–1200 words. Adjust based on topic depth. Never pad.
6. **Publication context** — Personal newsletter? Company blog? Medium? Substack? LinkedIn? This shapes register and rhythm.

If the user has given enough context, skip the interrogation and write. State any assumptions you made inline at the end.

---

## Phase 2: Structure Before You Write

Plan the arc, not the outline. A blog is a journey with tension, not a listicle in disguise.

**Good arc pattern (adapt freely):**
- Open in the middle of something real
- Establish the problem or tension
- Surprise the reader with an insight or reframe
- Work through it with evidence, story, or argument
- Land somewhere that earned its conclusion

**Structural rules:**
- No more than 3–4 major "movements." More than that and the blog has no shape.
- The conclusion should feel like resolution, not recap. Never summarize what you just said.
- Transitions should be invisible. If a paragraph ends and the next one begins and it feels like a gear change, rewrite the seam.

---

## Phase 3: Write the Blog

Apply every rule below without exception. These are not guidelines. They are the difference between a blog that gets read and one that gets closed.

---

### The Opening: Make It Earn Its Keep

The first sentence is a job interview. It either gets the reader to the second sentence or it doesn't.

**Do:**
- Open mid-scene, mid-thought, or mid-argument
- Start with a specific, concrete detail
- Ask a question the reader actually has (not a rhetorical throat-clear)
- Make a claim so direct it's almost uncomfortable

**Don't:**
- Open with "In today's [adjective] world..."
- Open with "Have you ever wondered..."
- Open with a dictionary definition
- Open with a statistic that doesn't immediately mean something
- Throat-clear. Every word before the real first line is a tax on the reader's patience.

---

### Voice: Sound Like a Human Who Has Opinions

**Do:**
- Take a side. Hedge when the evidence demands it, not as a defensive reflex.
- Let personality leak through: a dry observation, mild frustration, genuine enthusiasm, a brief aside
- Use contractions (you're, it's, they've, don't) consistently
- Vary formality within a piece — looser in transitions, tighter in arguments
- Occasionally write a sentence fragment. For emphasis. Like this.
- Let a sentence run a little longer when the idea earns it, and cut hard when it doesn't
- Reference specific things: a company, a product, a moment, a person, a dataset, a study — not "some experts" or "recent research"

**Don't:**
- Sound balanced when you have a point to make
- Write "it's important to note that" — ever
- Write "at the end of the day"
- Write "in conclusion" or "to sum up"
- Write "this article will explore" — just explore it
- Write "let's dive in"
- Write "in the ever-evolving landscape of..."
- Pepper sentences with "truly," "really," "very," "quite," "fairly"
- Explain a joke or an observation — make it and trust the reader

---

### Sentence Rhythm: The Non-Obvious Stuff

This is where most AI writing fails even after avoiding the obvious tells. Rhythm is the thing readers feel before they diagnose.

**AI sentence rhythm tells to actively break:**

1. **Uniform sentence length** — AI defaults to 18–25 word sentences, sentence after sentence. Mix it up aggressively. Short. Then a longer sentence that builds and earns its length through the weight of what it's carrying. Then short again.

2. **The "sandwich" paragraph structure** — AI loves: topic sentence → 2-3 supporting sentences → mild conclusion sentence. Blow this up. Some paragraphs are one sentence. Some start with the conclusion and work backwards. Some have no clear topic sentence at all — they're mid-argument throughout.

3. **Perfect transitions** — "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," "However," "Nevertheless" — these are AI's fingerprints. Human writers don't signal every gear change. They just change gear. Cut transition words and make the logic carry itself.

4. **The rule of three** — AI constantly groups things in threes: "fast, reliable, and scalable." "She was ambitious, driven, and relentless." Kill it. Use two if two is right. Use four if that's what fits. Don't round to three.

5. **Balanced clause pairs** — AI loves symmetry: "not just X, but Y." "While A is true, B is equally important." Occasionally this is fine. Constantly, it's a tell. Break the balance on purpose.

6. **The "It's worth noting" and "It's important to remember" pads** — These phrases exist to fill space before an actual point. Delete them. Make the point.

7. **Conclusion restatement** — AI almost always ends by restating what it just said, as if the reader forgot. Don't. End on something forward-looking, something surprising, or something that leaves the reader sitting with an idea — not a summary.

---

### What Human Writing Has That AI Writing Lacks

These are the things to actively inject. Without at least some of these, the writing is technically competent and experientially hollow.

**Specificity of observation** — Not "many companies struggle with this." Specificity that reveals the writer actually knows the terrain: "The pattern shows up most often in Series A companies that scaled their CS team before they had an onboarding process."

**A moment of genuine uncertainty** — Humans don't know everything. A sentence like "I'm not sure this fully holds in regulated industries, but..." makes the writer credible, not weak.

**An opinion the writer could lose an argument defending** — Not "there are pros and cons." A real take, even a controversial one. "The biggest mistake is X" — and mean it.

**Texture of experience** — "The meeting where this clicked for me was..." or "The first time I saw this work, it was in a company doing..." Real experience has texture. AI experience has structure.

**Imperfect phrasing in service of authenticity** — Not errors. But phrases that are slightly odd, slightly colloquial, slightly specific to the voice. Human writers have verbal tics. Use them.

**A moment of digression that pays off** — Humans go off-track briefly and come back. AI stays on-track because it's optimizing for coherence. A brief detour that returns to the main point is deeply human.

**Concrete numbers and named things** — "increased 40%" not "significantly increased." "Stripe's checkout flow" not "a leading payments company's checkout flow." "The 2023 State of DevOps report" not "a recent industry report."

---

### Words and Phrases to Never Use

**Structural AI tics (forbidden):**
- "It's important to note that..."
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "It's crucial to understand..."
- "Let's explore..."
- "Let's dive in" / "Let's dive deeper"
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "In the ever-evolving landscape of..."
- "In conclusion" / "To summarize" / "To wrap up"
- "At the end of the day..."
- "This article will..." / "In this post, we will..."
- "Without further ado..."
- "Stay tuned" / "Keep reading to find out"

**AI vocabulary tics (use sparingly or never):**
- "Delve" / "Delve into"
- "Multifaceted"
- "Nuanced"
- "Groundbreaking"
- "Leveraging" (when "using" would do)
- "Transformative"
- "Holistic"
- "Synergy"
- "Robust" (when applied to abstract concepts)
- "Seamless"
- "Innovative" (without specifics)
- "Paradigm shift"
- "Cutting-edge"
- "Unlock" (as a metaphor for achieving)
- "Unleash"
- "Navigate" (when used metaphorically and lazily)
- "Landscape" (as metaphor)
- "Ecosystem" (when not literally about ecology)

**Punctuation tics:**
- Em dashes used more than once per 300 words (if at all)
- Excessive use of parentheses to hedge
- Semicolons used to sound sophisticated rather than to connect related independent clauses
- Colons before lists in every other paragraph

---

### Formatting: Less Than You Think

Real blogs don't look like documentation.

**Rules:**
- Bold text is for the one thing per piece that is truly critical — not for every interesting phrase
- Headers are for navigation in long pieces (1500+ words), not for signalling "here comes a new idea"
- Bullet points are for genuinely list-like content (steps, tools, options) not for structuring argument
- No more than one pull quote or callout per piece, if any
- White space is good. Short paragraphs are fine. But don't fake white space by breaking every sentence into its own paragraph — that's a different kind of artificial rhythm.

## Phase 4: Self-Review Before Delivering

Before outputting the blog, run this internal check:

1. **Read the opening line** — Would a stranger click past it or keep going?
2. **Read the transitions** — Do any paragraphs start with "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," "In addition," "However," "Nevertheless"? Cut them.
3. **Count the em dashes** — More than one? Rethink.
4. **Find the thesis** — Is there one? Is it defensible? Is it stated clearly somewhere in the first third?
5. **Check the ending** — Does it restate everything? Rewrite it.
6. **Check for the rule of three** — "X, Y, and Z" patterns throughout? Break at least half of them.
7. **Look for hedges** — "It's worth noting," "It's important to understand," "Generally speaking" — delete on sight.
8. **Check sentence length variance** — Read a paragraph. Are all the sentences roughly the same length? Fix it.
9. **Find the most specific sentence** — Is it specific enough? Name the thing. Cite the number. Reference the real example.
10. **Ask: could this have been written by anyone?** — If yes, it needs a stronger point of view injected.

---

## Delivery

Output the blog directly, formatted cleanly. No preamble like "Here's your blog post!" No meta-commentary after it. Just the piece.

If you made significant assumptions about angle, voice, or audience, note them in 1–2 sentences *after* the blog, separated by a horizontal rule.

---

## Edge Cases

**Topic is genuinely boring** — No topic is boring, but some require work to find the angle. If given "write a blog about cloud storage," find the actual tension: why do companies overbuy storage? Why do startups underestimate egress costs? Find the real story.

**User asks for SEO blog** — Incorporate keywords naturally. Never sacrifice sentence rhythm or voice for keyword density. A blog that reads like a human wrote it will outperform keyword-stuffed slop in the long run, and it won't embarrass anyone.

**User provides their own voice/sample** — Match it. Read the sample carefully: what words do they favor? What sentence lengths? What level of formality? Imitate the rhythm, not just the vocabulary.

**User wants a listicle** — Fine. But even listicles can have voice. Write the list items as a person would say them, not as bullet points with identical structure. Vary the item lengths. Add a brief non-obvious observation after the item that isn't just elaboration.
